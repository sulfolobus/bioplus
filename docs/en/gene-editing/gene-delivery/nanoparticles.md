# Nanoparticle Gene Delivery

Nanoparticles as non-viral vectors provide safe and flexible delivery solutions for gene therapy.

## Types of Nanoparticles

### 1. Lipid Nanoparticles (LNP)

**Structure composition:**
- Cationic lipids: Bind negatively charged DNA/RNA
- Phospholipids: Form bilayer structure
- Cholesterol: Stabilize particle integrity
- PEG lipids: Prolong blood circulation

**Advantages:**
- Good biocompatibility
- Scalable production
- High safety
- No immunogenicity

### 2. Polymer Nanoparticles

**Materials:**
- PEI (Polyethyleneimine)
- PLGA
- Chitosan

**Characteristics:**
- Controllable degradation
- Functional modification

### 3. Inorganic Nanoparticles

- **Gold nanoparticles**: Functionalizable
- **Silica nanoparticles**: Biodegradable
- **Carbon nanotubes**: Efficient delivery

## Mechanism of Action

### Cellular Uptake

```
Nanoparticles → Cell membrane endocytosis → Endosomal escape → Release to cytoplasm
```

### Key Factors

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Particle size | 50-200nm optimal |
| Surface charge | Positive charge facilitates uptake |
| Targeting ligands | Improves specificity |

## Clinical Applications

### LNP in mRNA Vaccines

LNPs played a critical role in the COVID-19 pandemic:

| Vaccine | Protection Rate |
|---------|-----------------|
| Pfizer-BioNTech | 95% |
| Moderna | 94% |

### Gene Therapy

- **Onpattro**: First approved RNAi therapeutic drug
- **LNP-CRISPR**: In vivo gene editing

## Technical Challenges

### Targeted Delivery

- Insufficient tissue specificity
- Surface modification needed

### Scale-up Production

- Complex process
- High cost
